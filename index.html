01

1865–1877 — Cause

Reconstruction: A Brief Opening

After the Civil War, federal troops enforced new constitutional amendments. Black Americans voted, held office, and built schools and churches. 16 Black men served in Congress. But without land redistribution — the promised "40 acres" — the structural foundations of inequality remained.

Reconstruction Era 40 Acres & a Mule Black Codes
02

1877–1934 — Policy

Withdrawal, Terror & Legal Exclusion

Federal troops withdrew under the Compromise of 1877. Jim Crow laws institutionalized segregation. The Ku Klux Klan used targeted terror. Black land ownership — briefly rising to 14 million acres by 1910 — was dismantled through intimidation, fraud, and legal manipulation over the following decades.

Jim Crow Laws Racial Terror Black Land Loss
03

1934–1968 — Federal Architecture

The Federal Government Builds Segregation Into Housing

The New Deal's HOLC program created "residential security maps" — redlining. The FHA and VA backed mortgages almost exclusively in white suburbs. The GI Bill distributed record wealth, but Black veterans were systematically excluded. The federal government didn't just permit inequality — it constructed it.

HOLC Maps GI Bill FHA Policy
04

1968–Present — Persistence

Segregation Without Law, Inequality By Design

The Fair Housing Act passed in 1968, but the structural inequalities were already embedded: in neighborhood composition, property values, school-district boundaries, and family wealth. Property-tax funding for schools meant that formerly redlined neighborhoods received less — and continue to receive less — per student.

Fair Housing Act School Funding Today's Data
05

Present Day — Effect

Where the Chain Ends Up Today

Formerly redlined neighborhoods show measurably higher rates of asthma, diabetes, and heat exposure. Median Black household wealth is $24,100 vs. $188,200 for white households. Majority-Black school districts receive $1,800 less per pupil annually. The chain connects 1934 to this morning's news.

Health Data Wealth Gap Education Equity

    HOLC Residential Security Maps

    1935–1940

    Federal mortgage security maps graded neighborhoods A–D. "Hazardous" D ratings (red) applied almost universally to Black neighborhoods, denying federally backed mortgages for decades.

    The GI Bill (Servicemen's Readjustment Act)

    1944

    Created the American middle class — for white veterans. Administration through segregated agencies and banks meant Black veterans could rarely access homeownership, college, or business loan benefits.

    Southern state laws passed immediately after emancipation to restrict Black freedom, labor, movement, and legal rights — effectively recreating the conditions of slavery under a different legal framework.

    1877

    Backroom political deal that resolved the 1876 election by withdrawing federal troops from the South — effectively ending Reconstruction and handing control back to white supremacist state governments.

    May 31–June 1, 1921

    A white mob, aided by city officials and aircraft, destroyed 35 blocks of Greenwood — killing hundreds of Black residents and destroying $30 million in property. Survivors received no government compensation.

    1954

    Supreme Court ruling overturning Plessy v. Ferguson's "separate but equal" doctrine. Its implementation was undermined by white flight, private academies, and decades of resistance from state governments.

    1910–1970

    Six million Black Americans relocated from the rural South to Northern and Western cities — fleeing Jim Crow terror and seeking economic opportunity, reshaping American politics, culture, and demography.

Then → Now

Policy has a long tail

These aren't historical events that ended in the past. They are chains that run unbroken to today's news cycle.

Redlining → School Funding → Health Outcomes

Then 1935–1968

Federal maps drew a color line around American cities

239

U.S. cities where HOLC "residential security maps" graded neighborhoods — with Black and immigrant areas rated D (red), making federally backed mortgages impossible to obtain.

Source: Digital Scholarship Lab, University of Richmond, "Mapping Inequality" (2018). HOLC archive records, National Archives.

Now 2024

Formerly redlined neighborhoods still show measurably worse outcomes

$1,800

Less per student annually in majority-Black school districts vs. majority-white districts — a direct result of property-tax school funding in neighborhoods whose values were suppressed by federal policy.

Source: EdBuild, "Dismissed" (2019). Urban Institute, "Long-Term Effects of the Discriminatory Geography of Homeownership" (2023).

Reconstruction → Disenfranchisement → Voter Suppression

Then 1865–1877

Black men voted and governed at rates never again seen until the 1960s

16

Black men served in the U.S. Congress during Reconstruction. More than 600 served in state legislatures. The infrastructure of Black political power was built — then systematically demolished after 1877.

Source: Eric Foner, Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution (1988). U.S. House of Representatives Historical Office.

Now 2013–Present

After Shelby County v. Holder, 29 states passed new voting restrictions

29

States enacted new voter ID laws, polling place closures, and registration restrictions within years of the Supreme Court gutting the Voting Rights Act's preclearance requirement — disproportionately affecting Black voters.

Source: Brennan Center for Justice, "Voting Laws Roundup" (2021). Protect Democracy, "Shelby County v. Holder Impact Analysis" (2023).
