COINTELPRO:
The FBI's War on Black America

From 1956 to 1971, the FBI ran a classified program called COINTELPRO — Counterintelligence Program — specifically designed to "expose, disrupt, misdirect, discredit, or otherwise neutralize" Black political organizations. Its targets included the NAACP, Martin Luther King Jr., the Nation of Islam, and the Black Panther Party. Its methods included infiltration, forged letters, planted evidence, and coordinating police assassinations. This is why the Civil Rights movement did not produce lasting institutional change.

The Central Argument

The Civil Rights movement didn't just lose momentum — it was systematically destroyed by the federal government. COINTELPRO explains the gap between the victories of 1964–65 and the mass incarceration, poverty, and political suppression of the 1970s–present. The FBI didn't just surveil Black leaders. It forged letters to turn organizations against each other, fed false stories to the press, and in the case of Fred Hampton, coordinated his murder while he slept. Understanding COINTELPRO is not optional for understanding Black America today.

On August 28, 1956, FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover sent a memo to field offices authorizing a new secret counterintelligence program. Its initial target was the Communist Party USA, but within months Hoover expanded it to any organization he deemed a threat to the "established order." The first major Black target was the NAACP — the oldest and most mainstream civil rights organization in the country.

The program's stated goals, revealed in documents obtained by activists who broke into the Media, Pennsylvania FBI office in 1971, were explicit: "prevent the rise of a 'messiah' who could unify and electrify the militant Black nationalist movement." The memo specifically named Martin Luther King Jr., Stokely Carmichael, and Elijah Muhammad as potential "messiahs" to be destroyed.

"We must prevent the rise of a 'messiah' who could unify and electrify the militant Black nationalist movement. Malcolm X might have been such a 'messiah'... Martin Luther King, Stokely Carmichael and Elijah Muhammad all aspire to this position."

— FBI COINTELPRO memo, 1968 (declassified)
Tactics · 1956–1971
02
1956–1971

The Tactics: How You Destroy a Movement from the Inside

Nationwide
Infiltration
FBI informants joined organizations at every level — including becoming chapter leaders — to report on members, instigate internal conflicts, and sabotage operations.
Forged Letters
The FBI forged letters purportedly from one Black leader to another, designed to create suspicion and rivalry. A forged letter almost caused a violent confrontation between the Black Panthers and US Organization in California.
Fake News Plants
FBI agents wrote fake news stories and planted them in newspapers through friendly journalists, depicting Black leaders as thieves, sexual predators, or government collaborators.
Tax Harassment
The IRS was directed to audit and harass Black organizations and their leaders, draining resources and time from organizing work.
Anonymous Threats
FBI agents sent anonymous threatening letters to Black leaders and their families, and to employers of civil rights workers to get them fired.
Targeted Assassination
In the most extreme cases, the FBI coordinated directly with local police to kill Black Panther leaders. Fred Hampton was murdered in his bed in 1969 in an FBI-coordinated raid.
MLK · 1963–1968
03
1963–1968

Martin Luther King Jr.: "The Most Dangerous Negro in America"

Washington D.C. · Atlanta · Memphis

After King's "I Have a Dream" speech in August 1963, Hoover wrote in an internal memo that King was "the most dangerous Negro in America." The FBI immediately began a full-scale operation against him — wiretapping his phones, bugging his hotel rooms, and attempting to destroy his marriage by mailing recordings of his extramarital affairs to his wife Coretta.

In November 1964 — two weeks after King received the Nobel Peace Prize — the FBI sent him an anonymous letter with a recording of his private conversations and a note urging him to commit suicide: "There is but one way out for you. You better take it before your filthy fraudulent self is bared to the nation." This letter was approved at the highest levels of the FBI.

The Church Committee hearings in 1975 confirmed that the FBI had considered King a communist threat, had worked to block his receipt of an honorary degree from Marquette University, had tried to replace him as a Black leader with someone more "acceptable," and had conducted the most intensive domestic surveillance operation in American history against a single private citizen.

"He was the most dangerous Negro in America. We must mark King now, if we have not done so before, as the most dangerous Negro in America."

— William Sullivan, FBI Domestic Intelligence Division chief, 1963

Fred Hampton was the 21-year-old chairman of the Illinois Black Panther Party. He had built the Rainbow Coalition — a cross-racial alliance of the Panthers, the Young Patriots (poor white Appalachians), and the Young Lords (Puerto Rican youth) — that Hoover considered more dangerous than any individual leader. Hampton was also organizing a free breakfast program feeding hundreds of Chicago children daily.

FBI informant William O'Neal had infiltrated Hampton's inner circle, became his bodyguard, and provided the FBI with a detailed floor plan of Hampton's apartment, including where he slept. O'Neal also drugged Hampton with barbiturates the night before the raid.

At 4:45 a.m. on December 4, 1969, Chicago police — briefed by the FBI — raided the apartment. Ninety-nine shots were fired; 98 came from police. One came from inside. Fred Hampton was shot twice in the head at close range while unconscious in his bed. He was 21 years old. His fiancée, eight months pregnant, was in the bed beside him.

A federal civil suit in 1982 resulted in a $1.85 million settlement — the largest civil rights settlement in U.S. history at the time — paid by the city of Chicago, Cook County, and the federal government. The settlement was an implicit acknowledgment of the murder's illegality. No one was prosecuted.

Exposure & Legacy · 1971–Present
05
1971–Present

The Break-In That Exposed Everything — and the Accountability That Never Came

Media, Pennsylvania · Washington D.C.

On March 8, 1971 — the night of the first Ali-Frazier fight, when the FBI believed its agents would be distracted — a group of eight activists broke into the FBI field office in Media, Pennsylvania and stole over 1,000 documents. They mailed copies to journalists and Congress. The documents revealed COINTELPRO for the first time.

The Church Committee (1975–76) formally investigated and confirmed the program's scope. Senator Frank Church called it "a sophisticated vigilante operation aimed squarely at preventing the exercise of First Amendment rights of speech and association." The FBI was directed to end the program. No agent was prosecuted. No officer involved in Hampton's death was charged with a crime.

The legacy is structural. COINTELPRO destroyed the organizational infrastructure that would have been needed to translate the legal victories of 1964–65 into economic and political power. The Voting Rights Act passed. The organizations that would have organized Black voters to use those rights were infiltrated, divided, and destroyed. The vacuum was filled by street gangs — which the FBI had also helped create by forging letters between the Panthers and gang leaders, deliberately engineering confrontations.

Today, the FBI's domestic terrorism program uses many of the same techniques against Black Lives Matter, environmental activists, and Muslim-American communities. The program was never dismantled. It was renamed.

What This Explains

The Voting Rights Act passed in 1965. Black voter registration in the South rose dramatically. Yet by 1972, no major Black political organization had the infrastructure to consolidate those gains nationally.

COINTELPRO is the explanation. The organizations were destroyed before they could govern.
