Oregon's Black Exclusion Law: Free State, White State

In 1857, Oregon wrote a Black exclusion clause directly into its founding constitution — making it the only state admitted to the Union with a racial ban on residency. It was a free state: no slavery, no Black people. The demographic shape that law carved persists in Portland's neighborhoods, its wealth gap, and its whiteness today.

The Founding Document

Written into the Constitution

Oregon's 1857 constitution didn't just reflect racial prejudice — it codified it at the highest legal level. Three provisions worked together to make Black life in Oregon legally impossible.

Article I, Section 35 — Exclusion Clause
"No free negro, or mulatto, not residing in this State at the time of the adoption of this Constitution, shall come, reside, or be within this State, or hold any real estate, or make any contracts, or maintain any suit therein; and the Legislative Assembly shall provide by penal laws, for the removal, by public officers, of all such negroes, and mulattoes, and for their effectual exclusion from the State."
Ratified by Oregon voters, November 9, 1857. Oregon admitted to the Union, February 14, 1859. This clause remained in the constitution until Measure 14 removed it in November 2002 — 145 years after ratification.
1844
The Lash Law
Oregon Territory's first exclusion law required any Black person present to be publicly flogged every six months until they left. Amended to "hard labor" after public objection — not on humanitarian grounds, but because lashing was considered too barbaric to apply to people who were nominally free.
1857
Constitutional Ban
Oregon voters ratified the exclusion clause by a margin of 8 to 1. Simultaneously, they voted to ban slavery by 2 to 1. The message was explicit: this was not a pro-slavery state. It was a pro-white state. No enslaved people and no free Black people.
1866–1973
14th Amendment Farce
Oregon ratified the 14th Amendment in 1866, then rescinded that ratification in 1868 — outraged that the amendment forced Black citizenship. Oregon didn't re-ratify until 1973, 105 years after rescinding. It was a symbolic vote with no legal effect. The intent was fully on record.
The Chain
The exclusion clause didn't just reflect white preference — it structured demography. Who settled Oregon, who built equity through homeownership, who received GI Bill loans in Portland's suburbs — all of it flowed downstream from a decision made in 1844 and constitutionalized in 1857. Portland is one of the whitest major cities in America. That is not coincidence. It is causation.

Before Oregon was a state, it was a territory run by settlers who had crossed the continent to build something new — and they were explicit about what that something would exclude. In 1844, the Oregon Provisional Government passed the first Black exclusion law on the continent. Any Black person — free or enslaved — was required to leave. Those who refused could be publicly flogged every six months until they complied.

The law was amended before it took effect, not out of conscience but aesthetics: legislators decided public whipping looked bad for a territory claiming to stand against slavery. The replacement required Black residents to perform hard labor until they left. The intent was identical. The message was clear: Oregon was not just anti-slavery. It was anti-Black presence.

The motivation was partly economic and partly ideological. Many white settlers had come from slaveholding states and did not want to compete economically with Black workers, free or enslaved. Others were building what historian Darrell Millner called a "utopian white homeland" — a vision of the Pacific Northwest as a fresh start, explicitly racially configured. Slavery was seen as corrupting that vision because it brought Black people. The solution was to prohibit both.

"We are in favor of keeping our territory free of negroes, both slave and free, as we consider them a detriment to society and a curse to any country."

— Oregon Spectator editorial, 1849, reflecting settler consensus

At the time Oregon achieved statehood, there were approximately 120 Black residents in the entire territory — a population so small it posed no conceivable threat to anyone. The exclusion laws were not responses to a problem. They were the construction of a racial architecture before one was needed.

When Oregon drafted its constitution in 1857, three racial provisions went before voters simultaneously: a ban on slavery, a ban on free Black residency, and a ban on Black voting. All three passed. The anti-slavery vote was 75% in favor. The Black exclusion vote was nearly 88% in favor. Oregon's voters were against slavery largely because it would have introduced Black labor competition — not because they believed in Black equality.

The result made Oregon unique in American history: the only state admitted to the Union with an explicit constitutional ban on Black residency. Other states had sundown towns, hostile practices, and discriminatory laws. Oregon had all of those things written into its founding charter — the highest legal document in the state — before a single federal law had been passed on the matter.

The exclusion clause was never seriously enforced at scale. Enforcement would have required actively removing people. What it did instead was function as a message and a deterrent: this state is not for you. It shaped the calculations of Black families deciding where to go during the Great Migration. It shaped what Oregon felt like to the few Black people who were already there. And it remained on the books, constitutionally, for 145 years.

"Oregon is the only state in the union whose original constitution contained an exclusion clause barring free Black people from living there."

— Oregon Historical Society

Congress admitted Oregon to the Union on February 14, 1859, with full knowledge of the exclusion clause. No objection was raised. The clause was noted, acknowledged, and accepted as compatible with membership in the United States of America.

Ratified, Then Rescinded: The 14th Amendment Betrayal

1868

The 14th Amendment granted citizenship to all persons born or naturalized in the United States, directly overturning the Dred Scott decision and, in theory, nullifying Oregon's exclusion clause. Oregon ratified it in 1866. Then, two years later, Oregon's legislature voted to rescind that ratification — an act of defiance that had no legal effect (ratification cannot be undone once the amendment is enacted) but was entirely meaningful as a statement of intent.

The rescission was driven by anger that the amendment required Oregon to treat Black people as citizens. Legislators who had supported ratification were voted out. Their replacements made the reversal. Oregon's official position, expressed by its legislature, was that it did not want Black people to be citizens.

It took 105 years for Oregon to re-ratify. In 1973, the legislature voted to redo the symbolic ratification — not because it changed anything legally, but because Oregon's growing Black community, centered in Portland's Albina neighborhood, had organized and pushed for the acknowledgment. It was, at best, a gesture. The exclusion clause itself remained in the constitution for 29 more years.

The pattern — ratify under pressure, rescind in protest, re-ratify symbolically decades later when the cost of doing so is near zero — is a precise record of Oregon's actual stance on Black citizenship across a century.

The exclusion clause defined who came to Oregon. The GI Bill and federal redlining defined who could stay, and on what terms. When Black shipyard workers arrived in Portland during World War II — recruited by the federal government to work at Kaiser shipyards — they were housed in Vanport, a hastily built city north of Portland on the Columbia River floodplain. Portland's existing housing was closed to them by restrictive covenants and informal exclusion.

On May 30, 1948, the Columbia River dike failed without warning. Vanport was destroyed in hours. 18,500 people lost their homes. The Black residents of Vanport — who had no other options — were directed to Albina, Portland's one neighborhood without formal exclusion covenants. Albina became Portland's Black neighborhood not by choice but by elimination of alternatives.

While white GI Bill recipients received federally guaranteed loans to purchase homes in Portland's new postwar suburbs — Beaverton, Lake Oswego, Gresham — the Federal Housing Administration's underwriting guidelines rated Black neighborhoods as high-risk and refused to guarantee loans there. Black veterans who had served in the same war could not access the same benefit. They were locked into Albina's rental market as white families were building the equity that would compound for three generations.

"Portland is one of the most racially segregated cities in the West. That's not an accident — it's a policy outcome. The policies just had different names at different times."

— Community Alliance of Tenants, Portland, 2019

In the 1950s and 60s, urban renewal programs demolished large sections of Albina to build Memorial Coliseum and expand Emanuel Hospital. Black residents were displaced again, this time by the institutions of civic progress. The neighborhood that had been created by exclusion was then destroyed by development — with residents receiving minimal compensation and few relocation options.

In 2002 — 145 years after Oregon voters approved the exclusion clause — Oregon put Ballot Measure 14 to a vote: remove the Black exclusion language from the state constitution. It passed, 71% to 29%. The clause was finally struck. But the vote itself is a data point that demands attention: nearly three in ten Oregonians, in 2002, voted to keep a provision banning Black people from living in their state.

Proponents of the "no" vote argued various positions: that removing the clause was unnecessary because it was unenforceable, that it was a waste of resources, that it was performative. These arguments are worth noting because they are structurally identical to arguments that have accompanied every step of this history — the exclusion clause was unenforced; the 14th Amendment rescission had no legal effect; the re-ratification was just symbolic. The through-line is a persistent preference for keeping the record intact while denying that the record matters.

Today, Portland's Black population is approximately 6% — among the lowest proportions of any major American city. The median household wealth gap between white and Black Portlanders is one of the largest in the nation. The neighborhoods where Black Portlanders were once concentrated — Albina, North and Northeast Portland — have been gentrified at some of the highest rates in the country, displacing tens of thousands of Black residents since the 1990s.

The exclusion clause is gone from the constitution. The exclusion it produced is not gone from the city. Between the 1844 lash law and the 2002 ballot measure is not a story of one bad law. It is the story of a durable structure, maintained through different instruments across different eras, consistently producing the same outcome: a state, and a city, that was built to be white and has remained, substantially, white.

"We have never fully grappled with the fact that our progressive reputation was built on top of one of the most explicitly white supremacist legal foundations of any state in the Union."

— Oregon State Representative Janelle Bynum, 2019

Key Document

Oregon's 1857 constitution with the exclusion clause is preserved in the Oregon State Archives. The clause reads in full: "No free negro, or mulatto, not residing in this State at the time of the adoption of this Constitution, shall come, reside, or be within this State…"

The clause was not a dead letter upon ratification. It was the architectural blueprint for a demographic outcome that remains visible today.
