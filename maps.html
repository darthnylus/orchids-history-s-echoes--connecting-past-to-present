African Origins · 300 BCE–1600 CE
c. 1235
Empire Founded
Sundiata Keita founds the Mali Empire

At its peak, the Mali Empire controls more than half of the world's gold and salt trade. The Manden Charter — one of history's earliest human rights declarations — is proclaimed. Mansa Musa I would make it the world's largest economy by 1312.

c. 1327
Pilgrimage
Mansa Musa's hajj to Mecca destabilizes Mediterranean gold markets

Mansa Musa travels to Mecca with 60,000 people and so much gold that his charity causes inflation across North Africa and the Middle East for a decade. His wealth, adjusted to today, exceeds $400 billion. European chroniclers begin documenting Africa's extraordinary wealth.
Transatlantic Slave Trade · 1441–1808
1441
First Raid
Portuguese sailors capture 12 Africans on the West African coast

The first documented Portuguese slave raid. Within decades, the trade becomes systematic and industrialized — the largest forced migration in human history. 367 years of documented voyages follow.

Founding Moment
"20 and odd" enslaved Africans arrive at Point Comfort, Virginia

The first documented arrival of enslaved Africans in English North America. The 246-year clock begins. Every piece of American wealth built before 1865 includes the uncompensated labor of enslaved people — a fact that shapes the racial wealth gap today.

Resistance & Revolt · 1712–1841
1712
Revolt · New York
New York Slave Revolt — 23 enslaved people fight back in the heart of the city

Twenty-three enslaved people set fire to a building and fought off the colonists who came to put it out. Nine colonists were killed. In response, New York passed some of the most brutal slave laws in the colonies — and the pattern was set: every revolt was answered not with reflection but with more repression.

In thread: Mass Incarceration →
1739
Revolt · South Carolina
Stono Rebellion — the largest slave revolt in colonial America

Nearly 100 enslaved Africans marched south toward Spanish Florida, where freedom was promised. They were suppressed by the colonial militia. The immediate result: South Carolina's Negro Act of 1740, which stripped enslaved people of the right to assemble, earn money, or learn to read — the direct forerunner of antebellum slave codes across the South.

In thread: Stolen Labor →
1791
Revolution · Haiti
Haitian Revolution begins — the only successful slave revolution in recorded history

500,000 enslaved people rose against the French colonial system in Saint-Domingue. By 1804, they had defeated Napoleon's army and established Haiti as the first Black republic. The U.S. response: trade embargo, diplomatic isolation, and refusal to recognize Haiti for 58 years. France extorted Haiti for 150 million francs (equivalent to $21 billion today) as "reparations" for the loss of French slaveholders' property — payments that continued until 1947.

In thread: Transatlantic Slave Trade →
1811
Revolt · Louisiana
German Coast Uprising — 500 march on New Orleans in the largest U.S. slave revolt

Led by Charles Deslondes, approximately 500 enslaved people marched down the River Road toward New Orleans with drums, flags, and cane knives. Suppressed by the U.S. Army and militia. The heads of the executed rebels were mounted on poles along the road as a warning. This was the largest slave revolt in U.S. history — and it is barely mentioned in American textbooks.

In thread: Stolen Labor →
1831
Revolt · Virginia
Nat Turner's Rebellion triggers the harshest slave codes in U.S. history

Turner led 70 rebels through Southampton County, Virginia. The rebellion lasted two days before being suppressed. The response lasted decades: Virginia banned the education of enslaved people; Southern states criminalized teaching Black people to read; abolitionist literature was banned from the mails. The rebellion that sought freedom instead produced a legal architecture of ignorance that outlasted slavery itself.

Emancipation & Reconstruction · 1865–1877
1865
Constitutional Amendment
13th Amendment abolishes slavery — with a carve-out

"Except as punishment for a crime." That exception becomes the legal foundation for Black Codes, convict leasing, and eventually mass incarceration. The carve-out is not an accident — it is the mechanism by which coerced Black labor continues after emancipation.

In thread: Mass Incarceration →
1877
Political Deal
Compromise of 1877 — federal troops withdrawn, Reconstruction ends

In a backroom deal to resolve the disputed 1876 presidential election, federal troops are removed from the South. Black political representation collapses within a decade. The brief experiment of Reconstruction — which produced Black governors, senators, and congressmen — is over.
Jim Crow & New Deal · 1896–1968
1933
Federal Policy
HOLC creates neighborhood "security maps" — color-coded by race

The Home Owners' Loan Corporation surveys every city in America and grades neighborhoods A–D. Grade D = "Hazardous" = Black. These maps determine who can access FHA-insured mortgages and GI Bill loans for the next 35 years — locking Black families out of the greatest wealth-building opportunity in American history.

In thread: Redlining → Achievement Gap →
1944
Federal Law
GI Bill creates American middle class — and largely excludes Black veterans

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act offers college tuition, low-interest mortgages, and business loans to WWII veterans. Administered through segregated local offices, Black veterans are steered away from benefits. The result: a white middle class built on government subsidy, a Black middle class blocked from the same.

Present Day · 1968–Today
2013
Supreme Court
Shelby County v. Holder guts the Voting Rights Act

The Supreme Court removes the preclearance requirement that had prevented states with histories of voter suppression from changing voting laws without federal approval. Within hours, states begin passing new voter ID laws, closing polling places, and purging voter rolls — disproportionately affecting Black voters.
Today
Present Consequence
The racial wealth gap stands at 8:1 — wider than in 1968

Median Black household wealth: $24,100. Median white household wealth: $188,200. The gap has widened since the Fair Housing Act was signed. Every thread traced on this platform connects to this present-day consequence — the compounded result of deliberate policy choices made across 400 years.
