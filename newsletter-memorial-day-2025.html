Special Edition May 26, 2025 · Memorial Day

5 Things You Didn't Know
About Memorial Day

The first Memorial Day ceremony was held by freed slaves. Congress made it official 100 years later. Here's what the textbooks left out.

By The Chain · 5 min read

1

The very first Memorial Day ceremony was held by freed slaves after the Civil War

When Confederate troops evacuated Charleston, South Carolina, newly emancipated slaves dug up a mass grave of fallen Union prisoners. They gave them a proper burial in a new cemetery, complete with a whitewashed fence and a powerful inscription: "Martyrs of the Race Course."

On May 1, 1865, a crowd of 10,000 strong — mostly freed slaves and missionaries — gathered for a parade. Three thousand Black school children marched with bouquets of flowers, their voices ringing out with "John Brown's Body." Members of the 54th Massachusetts and other Black Union regiments performed double-time marches. Black ministers recited verses from the Bible.

"The first Memorial Day was all about paying homage to the men who died fighting to abolish slavery."

This ceremony — organized entirely by Black Americans one month after the war ended — predates the official "Decoration Day" by three years. It has been largely absent from mainstream Memorial Day histories for over a century.

2

The first official "Decoration Day" was held on May 30, 1868 — at Arlington National Cemetery

Ohio Rep. James Garfield — a former general and future president — addressed a crowd of 5,000. His words still resonate:

"Hither our children's children shall come to pay their tribute of grateful homage. For this are we met today. By the happy suggestion of a great society, assemblies like this are gathering at this hour in every state in the Union."

— Rep. James Garfield, May 30, 1868

After the speech, the crowd solemnly entered the cemetery to pay respects to the Civil War fallen. The date — May 30 — was chosen partly because it didn't fall on any existing battle anniversary, and partly because flowers were in bloom across most of the country.

3

It took a full century for Memorial Day to become an official national holiday

Congress made it official in 1968 with the Uniform Monday Holiday Act — a law that moved Memorial Day, Labor Day, Columbus Day, and Veterans Day to Mondays to give workers more three-day weekends.

Over those 100 years, the scope had expanded from honoring Civil War dead specifically to honoring all fallen U.S. service members. The staggering losses of World War I and World War II — 116,000 and 405,000 Americans killed respectively — made the national observance more urgent than ever. The date was also shifted: from the traditional May 30 to the last Monday of May.

4

A National Moment of Remembrance has been held at 3:00 PM local time every Memorial Day since 2000

Congress passed the National Moment of Remembrance Act in 2000, dedicating one minute — at 3:00 PM local time — to pause, reflect, and honor all who died in service to their country.

The act was championed by the White House Commission on the National Moment of Remembrance, which was established to help restore the solemn meaning of Memorial Day amid concerns that it had become primarily associated with the start of summer, retail sales, and barbecues rather than military sacrifice.

5

The iconic red poppy became a symbol of remembrance after World War I — inspired by a poem written in the trenches

The red poppy entered the language of remembrance through Canadian Lt. Col. John McCrae's haunting poem "In Flanders Fields" (1915), written after he witnessed the death of a close friend at the Second Battle of Ypres. The poem opens:

"In Flanders fields the poppies blow / Between the crosses, row on row, / That mark our place; and in the sky / The larks, still bravely singing, fly / Scarce heard amid the guns below."

— Lt. Col. John McCrae, 1915

Poppies had grown wild across the churned battlefields of Belgium and northern France — their red blooms emerging from the soil disturbed by explosions and mass burial. The flower became a living testament to the resilience of life amid the devastation of war.

American activist Moina Michael read the poem in 1918 and began wearing a red poppy in honor of the fallen — launching a campaign that spread the symbol internationally. Today, the red poppy is worn across the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia every November in remembrance of the war dead.

The Chain connection

Why the first Memorial Day was held by Black Americans — and then forgotten

The erasure of the May 1, 1865 Charleston ceremony from mainstream Memorial Day history is not accidental. As Reconstruction ended and Jim Crow began, Southern states created their own "Confederate Memorial Day" and pushed back against the Union-aligned origins of the holiday. The history of who actually started Memorial Day was gradually written out — replaced by a narrative that centered white Northern veterans.

David Blight, historian at Yale, documented the Charleston ceremony in his book Race and Reunion: The Civil War in American Memory (2001). His research restored the record. But the story remains largely absent from public school curricula and mainstream Memorial Day coverage — a small example of the larger pattern Chain documents: the systematic removal of Black agency and contribution from American historical memory.
