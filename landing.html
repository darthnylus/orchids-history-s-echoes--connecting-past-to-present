300 – 1600 CE

Chapter One

Sovereign Civilizations
ruled the earth.

Thriving empires, immense wealth, and vast knowledge flourished across Africa long before the invasions. The Mali Empire controlled half the world's gold supply. Timbuktu held 700,000 manuscripts. The Kingdom of Kush built more pyramids than Egypt. These were not primitive societies — they were the intellectual and economic centers of the world.

1441 – 1867

Chapter Two

The Transatlantic
Disruption.

Over four centuries, 12.5 million African people were forcibly transported across the Atlantic. But the slave trade was not the beginning of the story — it was the destruction of one. The political instability created by European gunpowder weapons, the collapse of West African empires, and the construction of a global economy built on stolen labor — these are the links in the chain that follows.

1865 – 1877

Chapter Three

Reconstruction —
the promise that flickered.

For twelve years after the Civil War, Black Americans held Congressional seats, built schools and churches, and began accumulating land and wealth. It was the closest America came to its stated ideals — and it was violently dismantled. The Compromise of 1877 withdrew federal troops. Reconstruction was killed by politics, not by time. Every promise made was deliberately broken.

1934 – 1968

Chapter Four

Redlining America.
By design.

The federal government drew maps dividing American cities into four grades. Grade D neighborhoods — marked in red — were predominantly Black. Banks refused to lend in them. The GI Bill sent white veterans to new suburban homes while Black veterans were locked out. The racial wealth gap was not an accident. It was policy. These maps still predict outcomes in 2024.
