City Histories · 1600s – Present

New York:
Built on Slavery,
Remade by Harlem

New York enslaved more people than any other Northern colony. It destroyed thriving Black communities to build Central Park and highways. Then Harlem happened. Then the South Bronx happened. And the world's culture was never the same.

Time span
1600s – Present
Central theme
Erasure and explosive cultural reinvention
Key figures
Rustin · Randolph · Baldwin · Kool Herc · Jay-Z
The Central Argument

New York presents itself as the liberal counterpoint to Southern racism — the destination, not the oppressor. The historical record is more complicated. New York was a slave city. It destroyed its own Black communities for parkland and expressways. It redlined its Black neighborhoods, concentrated its poor in towers, and policed them like an occupied territory. What New York actually produced — Harlem Renaissance literature, bebop jazz, hip-hop, the labor movement, modern civil rights strategy — came not because of the city's liberalism, but in spite of its structural violence, and often in direct response to it.

New Amsterdam & Slavery · 1600s – 1827
1
1626 – 1827

New York Was a Slave City — The North's Most Profitable Slave Port

Manhattan / New Amsterdam

The Dutch West India Company imported the first enslaved Africans to New Amsterdam in 1626 — the same year Peter Minuit allegedly purchased Manhattan from the Lenape. Slavery was foundational to the colony's economy from its first decade. When the British took control and renamed it New York in 1664, they expanded the slave trade. By 1703, 42% of New York City households owned enslaved people — the highest proportion of any city outside the American South. The New York slave market operated openly on Wall Street from 1711 until 1762. New York was, for over a century, the second-largest slave port in colonial North America after Charleston.

The city's financial infrastructure — its insurance companies, its banks, its shipping merchants — was built on Southern cotton backed by enslaved labor. Slavery's Capitalism (2016) and Edward Baptist's The Half Has Never Been Told document in detail how Wall Street's financial instruments (futures contracts, credit, insurance) were developed to price, leverage, and trade enslaved people as assets. New York State formally abolished slavery on July 4, 1827 — 36 years after Vermont, but the last Northern state to act.

In 1712 and 1741, enslaved New Yorkers organized resistance. The 1712 revolt killed nine white men before being crushed; 21 rebels were executed. The 1741 "Great Negro Plot" — whether a real conspiracy or a panic — led to the execution of 34 people (17 Black, 4 white) and the expulsion of 72 Black New Yorkers. These revolts predated the American Revolution and are almost entirely absent from New York's public history.

Seneca Village · 1825 – 1857
2
1825 – 1857

Seneca Village: The Thriving Black Community Destroyed for Central Park

West 82nd–89th Streets, Manhattan (now Central Park)

In 1825, a group of Black New Yorkers began purchasing land in a remote stretch of upper Manhattan, then rural farmland. By the 1840s, Seneca Village had become one of the most stable Black communities in New York City: roughly 260 residents, three churches (AME Zion, All Angels' Episcopal, and a Methodist chapel), a school, a cemetery, and gardens. Its most significant feature was land ownership — in a city where Black voters had to own property worth $250 to qualify to vote (a requirement that did not apply to white men), Seneca Village's landowners were among the relatively few Black New Yorkers who could exercise the franchise.

In 1853, the New York state legislature authorized the city to use eminent domain to seize the land for a new public park — what would become Central Park. The residents of Seneca Village were evicted by 1857. City newspapers and park boosters described the neighborhood as a "shantytown" and its residents as squatters — language that was factually false (residents held deeds) and served to justify erasure. The land was cleared, leveled, and landscaped. For over a century, Seneca Village's existence was not acknowledged in Central Park's official history. Archaeological excavations in the 1990s confirmed its extent.

→ The Chain from Here

Seneca Village was not unique. Throughout the 19th and 20th centuries, New York used eminent domain, zoning, and urban renewal to displace Black communities that had built stable institutional lives. The pattern — Black community-building followed by government seizure framed as public benefit — recurs from Seneca Village (1857) to the Cross Bronx Expressway (1955) to the present-day gentrification of Harlem.

Labor & Civil Rights Strategy · 1920s – 1960s
3
1925 – 1968

A. Philip Randolph and Bayard Rustin: The Architects of the Movement

Harlem, New York

Two of the most consequential architects of twentieth-century civil rights strategy were Harlem-based. A. Philip Randolph founded the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters in 1925 — the first major Black labor union in America, organized among the Pullman porters who were the backbone of Black middle-class employment. After twelve years of struggle against the Pullman Company, the union won its contract in 1937, achieving what no Black labor organization had managed before. In 1941, Randolph threatened a march of 100,000 Black workers on Washington unless Roosevelt desegregated the defense industry — a threat credible enough that Roosevelt signed Executive Order 8802, banning discriminatory hiring in federal contracts. Randolph organized the actual March on Washington in 1963.

Bayard Rustin, Randolph's deputy and the chief logistician of the 1963 March on Washington, was the man who made the civil rights movement operationally possible. He introduced King to Gandhian nonviolence after Randolph introduced them in 1956. He organized the Montgomery Bus Boycott's infrastructure. He planned the March on Washington for 250,000 people in six weeks. He was pushed to the margins of civil rights leadership because he was openly gay — a fact his colleagues exploited to sideline him whenever his prominence became inconvenient. The march he built made King's "I Have a Dream" speech possible. His name was absent from the podium.

"The only weapon we have is our bodies, and we need to tuck them in places so wheels don't turn."

— Bayard Rustin
→ The Chain from Here

Rustin was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom by President Obama in 2013. The Bayard Rustin High School for the Humanities opened in Chelsea, Manhattan in 1988. His erasure from civil rights history — specifically because he was gay — tracks the broader pattern of Black LGBTQ+ contributions being written out of the movement they helped build.

4
1964

The 1964 Harlem Uprising: The Rebellion That Predated the "Long Hot Summers"

Harlem, New York

On July 16, 1964, an off-duty New York City police officer named Thomas Gilligan shot and killed James Powell, a 15-year-old Black student, outside a summer school program on East 76th Street. Witnesses said Powell was unarmed. Three days later, the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE) organized a rally in Harlem to demand Gilligan's suspension. When police moved on the crowd, the uprising began. It lasted six nights and spread to Bedford-Stuyvesant in Brooklyn. One person was killed, 141 police officers and civilians were injured, 185 were arrested, and 112 businesses were looted or burned.

The Harlem uprising predates Watts (1965), Detroit (1967), and Newark (1967) — the events that dominate the narrative of "urban unrest" in the 1960s. It has been largely written out of that history, partly because it embarrassed the liberal narrative of New York as a model of racial progress, and partly because it occurred in the middle of the 1964 presidential campaign, when the Johnson administration needed to contain the political damage. A grand jury declined to indict Gilligan. No officer was disciplined.

→ The Chain from Here

The conditions that produced the 1964 Harlem uprising — police violence against Black youth, overcrowded segregated housing, unemployment in nominally integrated Northern cities — were the same conditions King encountered in Chicago two years later. The Kerner Commission (1968) would describe them as "two societies, one Black, one white — separate and unequal."

The South Bronx & Hip-Hop · 1960s – 1980s
5
1955 – 1977

Robert Moses, the Cross Bronx Expressway, and the Deliberate Destruction of the South Bronx

The Bronx, New York

Robert Moses, New York's unelected master builder who controlled public works from the 1930s through the 1960s, routed the Cross Bronx Expressway directly through the middle of the East Tremont neighborhood — a dense, working-class Jewish and Black community of 60,000 people — when cheaper, less destructive routes were available. Construction began in 1948 and was completed in 1963, displacing 60,000 residents and demolishing 60 apartment buildings. Moses's choice was not accidental: he consistently routed highways through poor and minority neighborhoods, whose residents lacked the political power to stop him.

The expressway severed the Bronx's social fabric. White middle-class residents accelerated their move to the suburbs. Landlords, facing declining property values and tenants who couldn't pay rising rents, discovered a profitable strategy: arson for insurance money. Between 1970 and 1980, more than 7 square miles of the South Bronx burned. Forty percent of the housing stock was destroyed. The population fell from 1.5 million to 1.1 million. President Carter visited in 1977 and compared the landscape to bombed-out Dresden. The city's official response was to withdraw services — firehouses, schools, sanitation — from neighborhoods deemed not worth saving.

What the city abandoned, its residents rebuilt — not with city funding but with creativity. The South Bronx in the 1970s, left to burn, produced the four foundational elements of hip-hop: DJing, MCing, b-boying, and graffiti art. The music that would become the most commercially dominant genre in the world was born in the ruins of deliberate urban policy.

6
August 11, 1973 – Present

DJ Kool Herc and the Birth of Hip-Hop: A Party in a Burning Bronx

1520 Sedgwick Avenue, The Bronx, New York

On August 11, 1973, Clive Campbell — known as DJ Kool Herc, a Jamaican-American teenager — threw a back-to-school party in the recreation room of his apartment building at 1520 Sedgwick Avenue in the West Bronx. Using two turntables and a mixer, Herc isolated and repeated the percussion break sections of funk and soul records — the short, intense instrumental passages where crowds went wild. He called the technique the "Merry-Go-Round" or the breakbeat. The young men who danced to these extended breaks became b-boys. The technique Herc pioneered that night is the structural foundation of hip-hop production.

Within five years, Afrika Bambaataa — a former gang leader who founded the Universal Zulu Nation to redirect gang energy into music and art — and Grandmaster Flash had expanded Herc's innovation into a full cultural movement. Flash developed the precise turntable technique (the "clock theory") that made DJing an instrument. Bambaataa used hip-hop explicitly as conflict resolution, replacing gang warfare with music battles. In 1979, the Sugarhill Gang's "Rapper's Delight" became the first rap record to chart nationally, introducing hip-hop to a mainstream audience that had no idea where it came from.

By the 1990s, New York hip-hop had split into two defining aesthetic poles: the storytelling fatalism of Notorious B.I.G. (Bed-Stuy, Brooklyn) and the Cold Crush precision of Jay-Z (Marcy Houses, Brooklyn). Both men grew up in public housing projects built during the same urban renewal era that destroyed Seneca Village and the South Bronx. The genre they perfected has generated over $10 billion in annual revenue — almost none of which returns to the Bronx neighborhoods where it was invented.

"Hip-hop is the last true folk art. It's working-class music made by people who had nothing — no instruments, no studios, no label deals. They used what they had: two turntables, someone else's records, and the street."

— Paraphrase of Grandmaster Flash, multiple interviews
→ The Chain from Here

1520 Sedgwick Avenue was designated a New York City landmark in 2007 — the same year many of the South Bronx's surviving residents were being displaced by the gentrification that followed the neighborhood's artistic reputation. The building that birthed hip-hop nearly became luxury condos. Tenant organizing prevented its conversion.

James Baldwin & the Intellectual Tradition · 1940s – 1980s
7
1924 – 1987

James Baldwin: Harlem, Exile, and the Literature of American Conscience

Harlem, New York → Paris → Saint-Paul-de-Vence

James Baldwin was born in Harlem Hospital in 1924, the eldest of nine children raised by a stepfather who was a Pentecostal preacher. He grew up in poverty in Harlem at the intersection of two of its central institutions: the Black church and the Harlem Renaissance's literary world. He became a teenage preacher at 14 and a published writer by his mid-twenties. In 1948, he left for Paris — not from indifference to America but because, as he later wrote, he needed distance to see the country clearly. Distance did not dim the analysis.

His 1963 essay collection The Fire Next Time — written as two long letters, one to his nephew and one about his meeting with Nation of Islam leader Elijah Muhammad — remains the most precise diagnosis of American race as a psychological, political, and moral crisis ever written. His novels — Go Tell It on the Mountain (1953), Giovanni's Room (1956), Another Country (1962) — explored simultaneously the burden of Blackness and the suppression of homosexuality at a time when neither subject was discussable in mainstream American letters. He was, as Toni Morrison described him, "the last, the most, the most prodigious of the great Harlem Renaissance writers."

→ The Chain from Here

Baldwin's FBI file ran to 1,884 pages. COINTELPRO monitored him as a security threat. He was never awarded the National Book Award or the Pulitzer Prize. The 2016 documentary I Am Not Your Negro (dir. Raoul Peck) introduced his work to a new generation — reaching audiences his books, always in print, had never stopped growing.

Present-Day New York · The Unfinished Chain
8
1990s – Present

Stop-and-Frisk, Gentrification, and the Displacement of Harlem

New York City

Between 2003 and 2013, the NYPD conducted over 5 million stop-and-frisk encounters — 88% of them targeting Black and Latino men, 88% of whom were found to have committed no crime. Federal Judge Shira Scheindlin ruled the program unconstitutional in 2013, finding it a pattern of "indirect racial profiling." Mayor Bloomberg appealed the ruling. Mayor de Blasio dropped the appeal upon taking office in 2014. The stops declined sharply — and crime continued to fall, disproving the police department's central argument for the program.

Simultaneously, Harlem's demographics shifted sharply. The neighborhood that had defined Black urban culture since the 1920s saw its Black population fall from 97,000 in 1990 to under 55,000 by 2019 as rising rents, luxury development, and the same displacement forces that took Seneca Village in 1857 pushed Black residents further from Manhattan. The corner of 125th Street and Lenox Avenue — once the geographic heart of Black America — is now lined with chain stores and luxury apartments marketed to transplants who have never heard of Langston Hughes.

→ The Chain from Here

The chain from Seneca Village (1857) to Harlem gentrification (2010s) is unbroken: New York City has consistently prioritized the real estate value of Black-occupied land over the communities that built it. The mechanism changes — eminent domain, urban renewal, policing, market-rate displacement — but the outcome is the same.

The City That Made the World and Erased the Makers

New York built its wealth on slavery, its parks on seized Black land, and its highways through Black neighborhoods. Then called itself liberal.

The culture that defined the twentieth century — Harlem Renaissance literature, bebop, hip-hop — was created by Black New Yorkers working in conditions of concentrated poverty, state surveillance, and deliberate disinvestment. New York didn't produce that culture despite its racism. It produced it in direct response to it.
