The U.S. Supreme Court has significantly weakened the Voting Rights Act of 1965, effectively gutting Section 2 and limiting protections against racial gerrymandering.
Key Supreme Court Ruling
On April 29, 2026, the Supreme Court issued a 6-3 decision in Louisiana v. Callais, striking down Louisiana’s second majority-Black congressional district as an unconstitutional racial gerrymander (Alito, Roberts, and other conservative justices in the majority) while dissenting justices, including Elena Kagan, warned it undermines decades of civil rights protections. The ruling reinterprets Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, which previously allowed courts to challenge electoral maps that diluted minority voting power even without proof of discriminatory intent. The Court now requires plaintiffs to demonstrate intentional racial discrimination, raising the legal bar for challenging unfair maps.

Historical Context
The Voting Rights Act of 1965, signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson, was a landmark civil rights law designed to eliminate racial discrimination in voting. Section 2 specifically prohibited voting practices that diluted minority voting strength, enabling challenges to gerrymandered districts and discriminatory election procedures. Over the decades, the Act helped increase minority representation in Congress from roughly 1,500 African American officials in 1970 to over 10,000 today.
