While The Green Mile is officially a work of fiction written by Stephen King, the narrative of John Coffey bears a striking and heartbreaking resemblance to the real-life tragedy of George Stinney Jr. Stephen King has never explicitly confirmed that he based John Coffey entirely on Stinney, but the parallels between the fictional story and the historical event are so profound that historians, legal scholars, and audiences frequently connect the two.

Here is the real history that mirrors the tragedy of The Green Mile.

The True Story of George Stinney Jr.
In 1944, George Stinney Jr., a 14-year-old Black boy living in the segregated mill town of Alcolu, South Carolina, was arrested for the murders of two young white girls, Betty June Binnicker (11) and Mary Emma Thames (7).

The girls had gone missing after riding their bicycles looking for wildflowers. They had briefly stopped to ask George and his younger sister if they knew where to find a specific flower. The next day, the girls' bodies were found in a ditch. Because George had been seen speaking to them, police arrested him.

The Interrogation: George was interrogated by white police officers in a locked room with no parents, no lawyer, and no witnesses present. The police claimed he confessed, though no written confession or transcript was ever produced.

The Trial: His trial was a catastrophic miscarriage of justice. It lasted less than three hours. His court-appointed lawyer, a local tax commissioner planning a run for political office, called no witnesses, conducted no cross-examination, and offered no defense. The all-white jury deliberated for only 10 minutes before finding the 14-year-old guilty.

The Execution: On June 16, 1944, just 81 days after the girls were murdered, George Stinney Jr. was executed by the electric chair. Standing at only 5 feet 1 inch and weighing 90 pounds, he was too small for the chair. The executioners had to use a Bible (often reported as a phone book) as a booster seat so his head could reach the electrodes. The adult-sized face mask slipped off during the execution, revealing his tearful face to the witnesses.

The Exoneration
Seventy years later, in 2014, a South Carolina judge vacated George Stinney Jr.'s conviction. The judge ruled that Stinney had not received a fair trial, noting that his confession was almost certainly coerced, his constitutional rights were entirely violated, and the legal proceedings were fundamentally unjust.

The Parallels to John Coffey
If you look at the narrative of The Green Mile, the echoes of George Stinney Jr.'s death are undeniable:

The Accusation: Both John Coffey and George Stinney were Black males found near the scene and falsely accused of brutally murdering two young white girls.

The Execution: Both were executed via the electric chair ("Old Sparky").

The Innocence: Both were entirely innocent, victims of a deeply prejudiced and flawed legal system that demanded a quick conviction over actual justice.

Fiction vs. Reality: There are notable differences between the two. In the film, John Coffey is a towering adult with supernatural healing abilities set in the 1930s, whereas George Stinney was a terrified 14-year-old child in the 1940s. However, The Green Mile serves as a powerful allegory for the racial terror, rushed legal lynchings, and systemic brutality that destroyed lives like George Stinney Jr.'s in the Jim Crow South.
