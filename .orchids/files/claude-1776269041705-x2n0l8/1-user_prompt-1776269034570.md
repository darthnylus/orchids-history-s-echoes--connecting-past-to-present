Here are some of the most prominent groups that used terror and intimidation to keep Black families out of white neighborhoods:

1. The Spook Hunters
Based primarily in South Gate and Huntington Park, the Spook Hunters were perhaps the most notorious white gang of the 1940s and 50s.

Tactics: They wore jackets with a logo of a caricature of a Black person being chased. They patrolled the borders of white neighborhoods to "protect" them from Black "encroachment."

Violence: They were known for firebombing homes of Black families who moved across the "color line" (often around Slauson Avenue) and engaging in "rat packing"—ambushing Black individuals who were caught in white areas after dark.

2. The KKK (Ku Klux Klan) in Southern California
While often associated with the Deep South, the Klan had a massive presence in Southern California, particularly in the 1920s and again in the 1940s.

Local Strongholds: They were highly active in Inglewood, Huntington Park, and Long Beach. In the early 1920s, the KKK actually controlled much of the Glendale and Anaheim city governments.

Terror Campaigns: They utilized cross-burnings on the lawns of Black families and local leaders. In the late 1940s, they were heavily involved in the intimidation of Black veterans returning from WWII who sought to buy homes in new developments.

3. The "White Homeowners’ Councils"
While these often functioned under legal-sounding names like the White Circle League or various "Improvement Associations," they frequently operated as organized gangs to carry out illegal acts of terror.

The Watts Border: Groups in the white neighborhoods surrounding Watts (such as South Gate) organized "neighborhood watches" that were essentially paramilitary groups.

The "Bombing" Era: Between 1945 and 1955, dozens of Black-owned homes in Los Angeles were bombed or set on fire. These groups acted as the "muscle" to ensure that the Restrictive Covenants (which barred Black people from owning land) were enforced through violence when the courts failed to do so.

4. The "Blue-Coat" Gangs (Police Vigilantism)
It is historically significant to note that during this era, the line between white street gangs and the LAPD (under Chief William H. Parker) was often blurred.

Many Black residents at the time described the police as the "most powerful white gang" in the city.

The police frequently looked the other way when the Spook Hunters attacked Black youth, or they actively participated in "shakedowns" and beatings to discourage Black families from entering "white-only" parks, beaches (like Inkwell Beach), and theaters.
