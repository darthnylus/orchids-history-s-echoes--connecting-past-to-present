Chicago is arguably one of the most important cities in the United States regarding Black history. From its very founding to its massive influence on global music, politics, and media, the city’s Black history is vast and deeply impactful.  Here are some of the most significant milestones, figures, and cultural movements in Chicago's Black history:The Founding of ChicagoJean Baptiste Point du Sable: Chicago's first permanent, non-Indigenous settler was a Black man. A pioneer and trader of Haitian and French descent, du Sable established a prosperous trading post at the mouth of the Chicago River in the 1780s, effectively becoming the city's "founding father."  The Great Migration & BronzevilleThe Great Migration (1915–1970): Chicago was a primary destination for hundreds of thousands of African Americans fleeing the Jim Crow South in search of industrial jobs and safety. This massive demographic shift transformed the city's landscape forever.  The "Black Metropolis" (Bronzeville): Due to racially restrictive real estate covenants, Black Chicagoans were forced to live in a narrow, segregated strip on the South Side. Despite these intense constraints, the community built a thriving, self-sustaining economic and cultural hub in the Bronzeville neighborhood, complete with its own banks, hospitals, and a vibrant arts scene.  Media & Publishing GiantsThe Chicago Defender: Founded in 1905 by Robert S. Abbott with an initial investment of just 25 cents, this newspaper grew to become the most influential Black weekly in the nation. It played a direct role in sparking the Great Migration by publishing train schedules, Northern job listings, and editorials urging Southern Blacks to leave the South.  Johnson Publishing Company: Founded by John H. Johnson, this Chicago-based empire published Ebony and Jet magazines. These publications became absolute staples in Black households worldwide and fundamentally shifted how Black American life was documented and celebrated in the media.  Political & Activism MilestonesThe Birth of Black History Month: In February 1926, historian Carter G. Woodson announced the creation of "Negro History Week" (the predecessor to Black History Month) at the Wabash Avenue YMCA in Chicago's Bronzeville neighborhood.  Ida B. Wells: The legendary investigative journalist, suffragist, and anti-lynching crusader lived and worked in Chicago for decades. In 1913, she founded the Alpha Suffrage Club in Chicago, which was the first Black women's suffrage club in Illinois.  The Chicago Freedom Movement: In 1966, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. moved into an apartment on Chicago's West Side to launch a massive campaign against housing segregation, slum conditions, and redlining. The violent, brick-throwing mobs he encountered in all-white Chicago neighborhoods exposed the deep-seated racism of the urban North.  Unprecedented Political Power: Chicago has been the launching pad for major political firsts. Oscar DePriest (the first Black congressman elected in the North after Reconstruction), Carol Moseley Braun (the nation's first Black female U.S. Senator), Harold Washington (Chicago's first Black mayor, elected in 1983), and Barack Obama (the first Black U.S. President) all built their political foundations in Chicago.  Arts, Culture, and InnovationMedical Breakthroughs: In 1893, Dr. Daniel Hale Williams performed one of the first successful open-heart surgeries in the world at Chicago's Provident Hospital—which he also founded as the first Black-owned and operated hospital in the United States.  The Birthplace of Gospel, Electric Blues, and House Music: Chicago is the undisputed incubator of several major music genres. It is the home of modern Gospel music (spearheaded by Thomas A. Dorsey and Mahalia Jackson) and electric blues (pioneered by legends like Muddy Waters and heavily recorded at Chicago's Chess Records). Later, in the late 1970s and 80s, Chicago's Black and Latino LGBTQ+ community birthed House music, heavily influenced by DJ Frankie Knuckles at the South Loop club, The Warehouse.  Soul Train: Don Cornelius created and debuted the iconic music and dance television program Soul Train in Chicago in 1970 before taking it to national syndication.
