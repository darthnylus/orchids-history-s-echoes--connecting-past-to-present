You are entirely correct on both counts. The association between Black Americans and watermelons is not a random coincidence; it is the direct result of a calculated propaganda campaign designed to punish Black economic independence after the Civil War.

Here is the historical breakdown of how the watermelon evolved from an African crop to a symbol of Black freedom, and finally, into a weaponized racist caricature.

1. The African Origins
The watermelon (Citrullus lanatus) is indigenous to Africa. Genetic and archaeological evidence traces its earliest cultivation to northeastern Africa, specifically in the regions of modern-day Sudan and Egypt, dating back over 4,000 years.

Watermelons were highly valued in arid climates because they could be stored for months and acted as natural canteens, providing a safe source of hydration. The fruit was brought to the Americas by European colonists and heavily propagated through the transatlantic slave trade, as slave ships often provisioned them for the journey due to their long shelf life and water content.

2. The Symbol of Black Independence (Reconstruction Era)
Following emancipation, newly freed Black Americans faced an immediate economic crisis. They had no land, no wealth, and were actively barred from most sectors of the economy.

Growing and selling watermelons became a crucial lifeline for several reasons:

Accessibility: Watermelons were relatively easy to grow in the Southern climate, requiring minimal capital or acreage to start a crop.

Profitability: They could be grown locally and sold in town squares or along roadsides, providing a direct, independent source of income.

Autonomy: For a brief period during Reconstruction, the watermelon became a tangible symbol of Black freedom. When a Black man sold a watermelon, he was working for himself, on his own terms, keeping his own profits, and rejecting the sharecropping system that sought to re-enslave him through debt.

3. The Weaponization of the Fruit
The sight of Black Americans achieving self-sufficiency and enjoying their freedom was deeply threatening to the white Southern social order. Because white Southerners could no longer control Black labor through chattel slavery, they sought to control the image of Black freedom.

To shame Black entrepreneurs and undermine their economic progress, white propagandists launched a campaign to completely invert the meaning of the watermelon.

The Narrative Shift: Instead of acknowledging the watermelon as a smart, profitable cash crop, white media began depicting it as a symbol of Black laziness and simplicity. The narrative was twisted to suggest that Black people were so simple-minded that they could be easily placated with a slice of watermelon and a little bit of rest.

The "Unclean" Caricature: Cartoons and advertisements began illustrating Black people, particularly children (the "pickaninny" caricature), eating watermelon in highly exaggerated, messy, and animalistic ways. This was used to push the narrative that Black Americans were inherently unclean and unfit for integration into mainstream, "civilized" society.

Devaluing Black Labor: By portraying the watermelon as a frivolous, silly fruit associated with laziness, white society successfully devalued the legitimate agricultural and entrepreneurial work that Black farmers were doing.
