Jim Crow Era · 1856 – 1915

Booker T. Washington:
The Accommodation and Its Cost

He built Tuskegee Institute. He fed 10 million Black Southerners in a period of terror. He was also the most powerful Black man in America — and he used that power to suppress Black political dissent, block anti-lynching legislation, and tell white America that Black people would accept second-class citizenship. The debate he started with Du Bois is still unresolved.

The Central Argument

Booker T. Washington is one of the most complicated figures in American history. He built an institution that educated thousands of Black people under conditions of violent oppression. He also made a deliberate strategic choice to accept disenfranchisement and segregation in exchange for white philanthropic support — a choice that has been debated as either pragmatic survival or catastrophic accommodation ever since. Understanding both sides of Washington is essential to understanding the full spectrum of Black political thought.

1
1856 – 1881

Born Enslaved, Built From Nothing: Washington's Rise

Virginia → West Virginia → Hampton, Virginia

Booker Taliaferro Washington is born enslaved in April 1856 in Franklin County, Virginia. His father is almost certainly a white man — possibly his enslaver. After emancipation, his family moves to West Virginia where he works in salt furnaces and coal mines as a child. He teaches himself to read. At 16, he walks 500 miles to Hampton Normal and Agricultural Institute in Virginia — arriving penniless, taking a job sweeping the entrance hall as his admission test, and staying to graduate in 1875.

In 1881, the state of Alabama is looking for a principal for a new "colored" school in Tuskegee. They contact Hampton, expecting to receive a white man. Hampton recommends Washington. He arrives to find no buildings, no equipment, and almost no money — just a broken-down church and a hen house. He borrows money, buys land, and has students make their own bricks to build their own buildings. Tuskegee Normal and Industrial Institute is built, literally, by the labor of its students. It becomes one of the most important Black educational institutions in American history.

2
September 18, 1895

The Atlanta Compromise: What Washington Offered and What It Cost

Atlanta, Georgia

On September 18, 1895, Washington delivers the speech that defines his legacy — and divides Black America. At the Cotton States Exposition in Atlanta, before a largely white audience, he offers what becomes known as the Atlanta Compromise: Black people will accept social segregation and political disenfranchisement in exchange for economic opportunity and education. His most famous line: "In all things that are purely social we can be as separate as the fingers, yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress."

White America — North and South — responds with thunderous approval. Northern industrialists like Andrew Carnegie and John D. Rockefeller begin pouring money into Tuskegee. Washington becomes the most powerful Black man in America, effectively controlling Black political appointments under Presidents Cleveland, McKinley, and Roosevelt. His network — the Tuskegee Machine — influenced Black newspapers, Black schools, and Black organizations across the country. Leaders who opposed his accommodationist line found their funding cut and their reputations attacked.

"Mr. Washington represents in Negro thought the old attitude of adjustment and submission. He practically accepts the alleged inferiority of the Negro race."

— W.E.B. Du Bois, The Souls of Black Folk, 1903
3
1895 – 1915

The Secret Washington: The Man Who Fought Segregation in Private

Tuskegee, New York, Washington D.C.

Here is the complication that makes Washington impossible to simply condemn: historians have documented that he secretly funded legal challenges to segregation while publicly accepting it. He provided anonymous financial support for lawsuits against Jim Crow railway cars, voter suppression, and peonage. He used intermediaries to hide his involvement from the white philanthropists whose support he needed. The man who told white audiences that Black people would accept separate but equal was simultaneously writing checks to lawyers challenging it.

Washington's defenders argue this is pragmatism at its finest — working within the constraints of a violent white supremacist system to extract maximum gains for Black people. His critics, beginning with Du Bois, argue that his public accommodation gave white supremacy a legitimacy it would not otherwise have had, that his suppression of dissent through the Tuskegee Machine damaged Black political development, and that his framework — emphasizing industrial education over higher education, economic advancement over political rights — left Black America without the political tools to defend its economic gains when they were inevitably attacked.

Both arguments are correct. Washington operated in conditions of extreme terror — Reconstruction had just been violently destroyed, lynching was epidemic, and overt resistance to white power typically meant death. His institution survived and educated thousands. His accommodation also provided cover for the construction of the Jim Crow system. This is the tension at the heart of his legacy.

Washington vs. Du Bois
Washington: Industrial education, economic self-sufficiency, accept social separation, avoid confrontation with white power

Du Bois: Liberal arts education, political rights, civil equality, direct confrontation with white supremacy

The debate they represent — accommodation vs. resistance, pragmatism vs. principle — runs through Black political thought from 1895 to the present.
Tuskegee's Legacy
George Washington Carver did his groundbreaking agricultural research at Tuskegee

The Tuskegee Airmen trained there

Tuskegee University continues today as one of the nation's leading HBCUs

The Tuskegee Syphilis Study — the most notorious medical ethics violation in U.S. history — was also conducted there
Accommodation and Its Costs

He built Tuskegee. He also told white America that Black people would accept second-class citizenship.

Washington's legacy is genuinely contested — not because the history is unclear but because the strategic question he answered is still unanswered: when faced with overwhelming violent power, do you accommodate or resist? Both choices have consequences.
