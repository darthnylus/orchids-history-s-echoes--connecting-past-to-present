Fried Chicken: African Technique, Black Enterprise, Stolen Industry

The frying technique that defines American fried chicken is West African. The spicing, the seasoning, the cast-iron method — brought across the Atlantic by enslaved people who were forced to cook it for others and denied the profits. After emancipation, Black women built a commercial fried chicken economy and were driven out of it by the same propaganda campaign that weaponized watermelon. Then Colonel Sanders industrialized the tradition, collected the credit, and made billions.

The Technique Lineage

How the Method Traveled and Who Got the Credit

The full history of fried chicken in America is a story of technique developed in Africa, labor extracted in slavery, enterprise built in freedom, and profit captured by white industry. Each step is documented. The credit was never distributed to match.

The cuisine was theirs. The profits were not.

The Chain
This is the same story as watermelon, patents, rock and roll, and every other Black innovation that became white industry. The pattern is consistent: Black people develop a technique or cultural product over generations → forced or coerced to produce it for others' profit → build independent commerce with it after emancipation → driven out of that commerce by targeted propaganda → white entrepreneurs commercialize the tradition at scale → the originators are left with the stereotype and none of the equity. The fried chicken industry is worth $28 billion. The Black women who built it before it was an industry are worth a paragraph in a food history book, if that.

Scottish immigrants brought a tradition of pan-frying chicken in fat to the American South. That fact is often used to claim that fried chicken is a Scottish-American invention. It is a partial truth that obscures the whole one. The spiced, seasoned, deep-fried chicken that became "Southern fried chicken" — and that became the basis of a global industry — is not the Scottish tradition. It is the African tradition, modified for American ingredients.

West African cooking had used deep frying in palm oil for centuries before the slave trade. The technique of coating chicken in seasoned flour or meal, then submerging it in hot fat to cook, was an established practice in Yoruba, Akan, and other West African culinary cultures. Enslaved people transported to the American South brought this knowledge with them — carrying it as one of the few possessions that couldn't be taken away. In the plantation kitchen, they adapted: palm oil became lard, local peppers and spices replaced West African ones, cast iron replaced clay cooking vessels. The technique remained African.

Enslaved women were the cooks. On most Southern plantations, the kitchen was one of the few spaces where Black women exercised real skill and some degree of authority — not freedom, but expertise. They fed both the enslaved community and the enslaving household. The culinary tradition they developed was sophisticated, labor-intensive, and the direct foundation of what the white South would later claim as its own regional cuisine. The women who built it received no credit, no wages, and no right to the dish they had refined.

"The enslaved cook stood at the foundation of Southern cooking. She brought her African knowledge of seasoning, frying, and technique into kitchens she did not own, to produce food she could not freely sell, for a cuisine that would later be attributed entirely to white Southern tradition."

— Adrian Miller, Soul Food: The Surprising Story of an American Cuisine, One Plate at a Time, 2013

The term "soul food" itself was coined in the 1960s partly to reclaim this history — to name the African American culinary tradition as a distinct and proud heritage rather than letting it continue to be absorbed into a generic "Southern" identity that erased its origins. But by then, the commercialization of the tradition had already begun.

1870s – early 1900s

After emancipation, one of the most documented examples of Black women building independent commercial power from food happened at the railroad junction in Gordonsville, Virginia. The town sat at the intersection of two major rail lines, making it a stop where passenger trains paused briefly. Black women — most of them formerly enslaved — seized that opportunity. They cooked fried chicken and other foods, carried them in baskets to the train platform, and sold directly to passengers through open car windows.

The "waiter carriers" of Gordonsville became famous enough to be documented in Northern newspapers and travel accounts. Passengers who passed through the junction specifically anticipated the food. The women developed reputations, repeat customers, and genuine income. They were operating small independent businesses — buying ingredients, cooking product, transporting it, pricing it, selling it — with no intermediary between their labor and their earnings. This was exactly the economic independence that the sharecropping system was designed to prevent.

The waiter carrier trade was not unique to Gordonsville. Similar economies existed at rail stops and market squares across the South wherever Black women could access a customer base and a product with low startup cost. Fried chicken was ideal: it traveled well, held heat, could be prepared in advance, and was genuinely desired by buyers. It was, like watermelon for Black male vendors, a route around debt peonage.

"The women of Gordonsville understood something fundamental: they had a skill, they had a product, they had customers passing through on a schedule. What they built from that was not informal or incidental. It was commerce."

— Kelley Fanto Deetz, food historian, on the Gordonsville waiter carrier tradition

The trade persisted into the early 20th century before declining — partly due to changes in rail travel, partly due to increasing legal and social pressure on Black public commerce. The women who built it are not household names. They left no corporations. The tradition they maintained, however, did not disappear. It continued in Black-owned restaurants, in church suppers, in the "Sunday chicken" culture that persisted through the Jim Crow era — and that Colonel Sanders entered when he opened his roadside stand in Kentucky in the 1930s.

The Propaganda Campaign: The Mammy and the Caricature

The propaganda campaign against Black fried chicken commerce ran parallel to and intertwined with the watermelon campaign. It used a different stereotype — the "Mammy" figure — but served the same function: reframe Black culinary expertise as a racial characteristic rather than a professional skill, and thereby devalue Black food enterprise and undermine Black commercial presence in public markets.

The Mammy stereotype depicted Black women as large, asexual, uniformly cheerful domestic servants defined entirely by their cooking and their devotion to white households. The stereotype was pernicious specifically because it contained a backhanded acknowledgment: yes, Black women were extraordinary cooks. But by framing that skill as natural, instinctive, and inseparable from servitude, it denied the expertise its economic value. A Black woman's fried chicken was "just something she does" — not a professional product deserving professional compensation. It could not be a business, only a service. It could not generate profit for her, only pleasure for others.

The Aunt Jemima brand, launched in 1889, is the clearest commercial example of this mechanism. The brand literally sold Black women's cooking knowledge — packaged as pancake mix, eventually expanding to syrup and other products — using the Mammy caricature as its marketing image. The original "Aunt Jemima" character was performed at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago by Nancy Green, a formerly enslaved woman from Kentucky who was paid to cook pancakes while embodying the character. Green was a gifted cook and a skilled performer. The corporation that owned the brand built a fortune on her labor and image. She owned none of it.

Colonel Sanders: The Franchise of a Stolen Tradition

Harland Sanders began selling fried chicken from a service station in Corbin, Kentucky in the 1930s. Corbin was a deeply segregated town — it was, in fact, a documented sundown town, a place where Black residents were not permitted to remain after dark. The Black culinary community whose techniques surrounded and informed Sanders' work was one he was in proximity to but that he could not have acknowledged as his source even if he had wanted to, given the racial dynamics of the era and region.

Sanders developed what he called his "Original Recipe" — a blend of 11 herbs and spices applied to chicken before pressure frying. He franchised the method in 1952, building Kentucky Fried Chicken into a national chain before selling it in 1964 for $2 million. The brand's origin story centers entirely on Sanders: the Colonel, the white suit, the string tie, the Kentucky roadside origin. The Black culinary tradition he cooked within — in Kentucky, in the American South, in a cuisine built by enslaved women across two centuries — is absent from the narrative.

"We have to ask: who built this cuisine, who refined it, who sold it commercially first, and who got rich from it? The answers to those four questions are not the same person."

— Adrian Miller, The President's Kitchen Cabinet, 2017

This is not an argument that Sanders invented nothing or that his business acumen was not real. It is an argument about credit, origin, and the distribution of economic reward. The people who developed the technique over centuries were not compensated. The people who built the first commercial fried chicken economy — the waiter carriers, the roadside cooks, the church supper organizers — did not become millionaires. The person who franchised the product they had developed did. The technique traveled from West Africa to American plantations to Reconstruction-era entrepreneurship to a fast food empire without the originators receiving equity at any stage.

Present Day

The Stereotype Stays. The Industry Grows. The Origin Is Unacknowledged.

The present-day situation is a precise summary of the full history. The global fried chicken industry — KFC, Popeyes, Chick-fil-A, and hundreds of regional chains — is worth tens of billions of dollars annually. It is built on a technique that originated in West Africa, was refined by enslaved Black women in American plantation kitchens, was commercialized first by Black women entrepreneurs in the Reconstruction era, and was franchised into a global industry by a white businessman in the 1950s. At no stage in that chain did the originators receive equity proportional to their contribution.

Simultaneously, the racist stereotype — Black people and fried chicken as a mark of ridicule — remains in active circulation. The same food that generated billions in white corporate profit is used to demean the people whose technique made that profit possible. The stereotype and the industry coexist without apparent contradiction because the industry has successfully severed its product from its origin story. KFC does not advertise in Gordonsville, Virginia. It does not credit West African frying traditions. It does not mention the waiter carriers.

Soul food restaurants — the Black-owned establishments that have carried the tradition from emancipation through the present — are facing their own version of the same suppression dynamic. In cities where Black neighborhoods have been gentrified, soul food restaurants are being displaced alongside their communities, often replaced by upscale restaurants selling "elevated Southern cuisine" — the same food, stripped of its cultural origin, priced for a white professional clientele, and called something else. The pattern does not require a conspiracy. It requires only a consistent economic logic: extract value from Black cultural production, strip the credit, and capture the profit.

"You cannot separate the fried chicken from the hands that perfected it, from the kitchens where it was built, from the women who sold it on train platforms. When you eat that food without knowing that history, you are eating someone's erased legacy."

— Psyche Williams-Forson, Building Houses Out of Chicken Legs: Black Women, Food, and Power, 2006
