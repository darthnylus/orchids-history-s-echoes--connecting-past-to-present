COINTELPRO: The FBI's War on Black America

From 1956 to 1971, the FBI ran a classified program specifically designed to "expose, disrupt, misdirect, discredit, or otherwise neutralize" Black political organizations. Its targets included the NAACP, Martin Luther King Jr., the Nation of Islam, and the Black Panther Party. Its methods included infiltration, forged letters, planted evidence, and coordinating police assassinations. This is why the Civil Rights movement did not produce lasting institutional change.

August 28, 1956

FBI Headquarters, Washington D.C.

Hoover's Memo: "Expose, Disrupt, Neutralize"

15 yrsOfficial duration 2,000+Documented operations

On August 28, 1956, FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover sent a memo to field offices authorizing a new secret counterintelligence program. Its first major Black target was the NAACP — the oldest and most mainstream civil rights organization in the country.

The program's stated goal: "prevent the rise of a 'messiah' who could unify and electrify the militant Black nationalist movement." The language was not accidental. It reveals that what the FBI feared was not crime — it was organized political power.

1956 – 1971

Nationwide

The Tactics: How You Destroy a Movement from the Inside

Infiltration Forged Letters Fake News Plants Assassination

FBI informants joined organizations at every level, reporting on membership, strategy, and internal tensions — then actively working to inflame them. The FBI forged letters between Black leaders to manufacture personal feuds and political splits.

In the most extreme cases, the FBI coordinated directly with local police departments to physically eliminate Black Panther leadership. The program did not surveil threats. It manufactured them, then used them to justify lethal force.

1963 – 1968

Washington D.C. · Atlanta · Memphis

Martin Luther King Jr.: "The Most Dangerous Negro in America"

After King's "I Have a Dream" speech, Hoover wrote internally that King was "the most dangerous Negro in America." This was not a fringe opinion — it was official FBI policy. The Bureau wiretapped his phones, bugged his hotel rooms across the country, and compiled a dossier designed to destroy his credibility with the Kennedy and Johnson administrations.

"In the fall of 1964, the FBI sent King an anonymous letter with a recording of alleged extramarital activity, accompanied by a note urging him to commit suicide. The letter was approved at the highest levels of the FBI."

King's surveillance did not end with his assassination in 1968. The FBI continued monitoring his associates for years afterward, treating the legacy of his movement as an ongoing threat to national security.

December 4, 1969

Chicago, Illinois

The Assassination of Fred Hampton

21Hampton's age 4:45 amTime of raid 99Shots fired (1 by occupants)

Fred Hampton was the 21-year-old chairman of the Illinois Black Panther Party. He had organized a cross-gang truce on Chicago's South Side and was building a coalition that united Black, Puerto Rican, and poor white communities. The FBI identified him as a future national leader — a potential "messiah."

FBI informant William O'Neal had infiltrated Hampton's inner circle and provided agents with a detailed floor plan of his apartment, including the location of his bed. At 4:45 a.m. on December 4, Chicago police — briefed and directed by the FBI — raided the apartment. 99 shots were fired into the apartment; 98 came from police. Fred Hampton was shot twice in the head at close range while sleeping. He was 21.

1971 – Present

Media, Pennsylvania → Congress → Now

The Break-In That Exposed Everything

In March 1971, a group of eight activists calling themselves the Citizens' Commission to Investigate the FBI broke into the Bureau's field office in Media, Pennsylvania, and stole over 1,000 classified documents. They mailed the files to journalists. It was the first the public had heard the word COINTELPRO.

The Senate's Church Committee confirmed the full scope of the program in 1975. It documented assassinations coordinated with local police, forged letters, psychological warfare, and the systematic destruction of Black political organizations. No FBI agent was ever prosecuted. No legislation was passed to prevent it from happening again.

"The program was not a policy failure or a rogue operation. It was the policy. The question is not whether it was dismantled — it was renamed."
