Jim Crow & Apartheid · 1890s–Present

Jazz & Blues:
Black Innovation and the Theft of American Music

Jazz and Blues are Black inventions — the most original American contributions to world music, born in the Mississippi Delta, New Orleans, and Chicago, created by Black musicians navigating segregation, poverty, and exclusion. They became the foundation of virtually every popular music form of the 20th century: rock and roll, soul, R&B, hip-hop. The people who created them rarely owned the masters, rarely received the royalties, and were regularly replaced by white artists who reached wider audiences playing the same music. The pattern has repeated across every decade.

The Central Argument

The history of American popular music is the history of Black cultural innovation followed by white commercial exploitation. This is not a metaphor — it is a documented economic and legal pattern. Black artists created forms; white artists recorded the same songs for white audiences and received airplay, record deals, and royalties denied to Black originators. Record contracts paid Black artists a fraction of actual sales. The music industry structure that extracted Black labor while denying ownership was not fundamentally different from the sharecropping system that preceded it.

Origins · 1890s–1920s
01
1890s–1920s

Where It Came From: The Blues, the Delta, New Orleans

Mississippi Delta · New Orleans, Louisiana

The Blues emerged from the Mississippi Delta in the 1890s — from the field hollers and work songs of Black sharecroppers, from the spirituals of the Black church, from the musical traditions brought from West Africa and transformed across two centuries of slavery and its aftermath. Robert Johnson, Bessie Smith, Son House, Charlie Patton: they documented a world of poverty, desire, grief, and endurance that white American culture refused to acknowledge existed.

Jazz was born in New Orleans — specifically in Storyville, the city's segregated entertainment district — from the convergence of blues, ragtime, and the rich Creole musical traditions of Louisiana. Louis Armstrong, Jelly Roll Morton, King Oliver: they invented an entirely new musical language. When the Great Migration carried Black musicians to Chicago, New York, and the West Coast, they carried it with them.

The Theft · 1920s–1960s
02
1920s–1960s

"Race Records" and the Cover Version Economy

United States

The recording industry in the 1920s and 1930s created a separate category called "Race Records" — music made by Black artists for Black audiences, sold in Black neighborhoods, played on Black radio stations. The industry's assumption was that white Americans would not listen to Black music. But white Americans did listen — quietly at first, then loudly. The industry's solution was the "cover version": take a Black artist's song, record it with a white artist, market it to white audiences, and let the white artist collect the commercial rewards while the original is locked in "Race Records."

This pattern became explicit in the 1950s with rock and roll: Pat Boone covered Fats Domino and Little Richard; Georgia Gibbs covered LaVern Baker; The Crew-Cuts covered The Chords. In each case, the white cover version was more commercially successful despite being inferior, because it had access to white radio play, white retail distribution, and white audiences that segregation blocked Black originals from reaching. Elvis Presley's relationship to Black music — deeply influenced, commercially successful, generationally wealthy — is the most famous example of a pattern that operated across the entire industry structure.

"Whole note of music I have seen coming out of Elvis Presley came from us. We created it. They took it from us."

— Little Richard, 1990
03
1950s–Present

The Continuing Pattern: Rock, Hip-Hop, and Streaming

United States

Rock and roll was invented by Black artists. The British Invasion — the Beatles, Rolling Stones, Led Zeppelin — was explicitly derived from Black American blues and R&B. The Stones and Zeppelin covered Black artists without credit or adequate compensation. The pattern replicated itself: Black artists created the form; international commercial success accrued to white artists who adopted it.

Hip-hop — invented in the Bronx in the late 1970s by DJ Kool Herc, Afrika Bambaataa, and Grandmaster Flash — followed the same arc. Black artists created the form in conditions of urban poverty and neglect. The industry structure that monetized it was controlled by white executives. The streaming economy that replaced physical sales has produced its own inequities: streaming royalty structures pay fractions of a cent per play and favor catalog ownership — which historically Black artists have had stripped from them — over current output.

The pattern is not historical. It is structural. As long as the ownership of distribution infrastructure, publishing rights, and master recordings remains concentrated in predominantly white corporate hands, the economic structure of Black musical innovation and white commercial extraction will continue — regardless of which specific genre is being extracted from.

The Cover Version Economy

Hound Dog: Big Mama Thornton (1952) vs. Elvis Presley (1956)

Tutti Frutti: Little Richard (1955) vs. Pat Boone (1956)

La Bamba: Traditional Mexican-American vs. Ritchie Valens vs. Los Lobos

In nearly every case: original Black artist, covered by white artist, white version outsells original due to radio access and distribution.
