City Histories · 1900s – Present

Detroit:
Motown, the '67 Uprising,
and the City They Left to Die

Detroit's Black community built Paradise Valley, invented the most commercially successful Black music empire in history, survived two devastating uprisings — and then watched the auto industry leave, the white population flee, and the state of Michigan drain the city dry before a bankruptcy judge sold it off.

Time span
1900s – Present
Central theme
Industrial promise, racist extraction, and abandonment
Key figures
Berry Gordy · Coleman Young · Aretha Franklin
The Central Argument

Detroit is the sharpest example in American urban history of what happens when a city's economy is built on a single industry that can leave, when that industry's workforce is racially segregated by union policy and residential covenant, and when the state withdraws investment from the communities left behind. Detroit's Black community did not create Detroit's collapse. They were its first victims — excluded from the prosperity of the auto boom by union racism and housing covenants, then blamed for the city's decline when the industry and the white population both abandoned it simultaneously.

Black Bottom & Paradise Valley · 1910s – 1950s
1
1910s – 1950s

Black Bottom and Paradise Valley: Detroit's Black Metropolis

East Side, Detroit

As Black migrants arrived from the South during and after World War I, Detroit's housing market confined them — through racially restrictive covenants, redlining, and violence — to a narrow corridor on the city's east side. The neighborhood's low-lying geography gave it the name Black Bottom; the commercial corridor along Hastings Street that served it was called Paradise Valley. By the 1940s, Paradise Valley was the most vibrant Black entertainment and commercial district in the Midwest outside of Chicago's Bronzeville — comparable to Harlem in its density of jazz clubs, theaters, restaurants, and Black-owned businesses.

The Graystone Ballroom and the Forest Club hosted every major Black musician of the era. Black Detroit produced or nurtured Ella Fitzgerald, John Lee Hooker, and the early careers of nearly everyone who would later record for Motown. The district had its own hotels (the Gotham Hotel, the only first-class hotel in Detroit that admitted Black guests), its own newspapers, its own professional class — doctors, lawyers, business owners who had been excluded from white institutions and built their own. In 1950, an estimated 120,000 Black Detroiters lived in this corridor.

→ The Chain from Here

Between 1950 and 1966, Black Bottom and Paradise Valley were completely demolished by urban renewal and freeway construction — specifically the Chrysler Freeway (I-375), routed directly through the neighborhood. 129 blocks were cleared. 8,000 families were displaced with minimal compensation. The community's institutions, its businesses, its social fabric — gone. The freeway is currently being removed by the city of Detroit, and there is an active effort to rebuild the neighborhood. It is one of the only cases in American history of a city formally attempting to reverse an act of racist urban destruction.

The 1943 Uprising · Race and the Auto Industry
2
June 1943

The 1943 Detroit Race Riot: When the Arsenal of Democracy Turned on Itself

Belle Isle and throughout Detroit

Detroit in 1943 was the "Arsenal of Democracy" — its auto plants had converted to wartime production and were employing hundreds of thousands of workers. Black workers had migrated in massive numbers for these jobs, but the United Auto Workers union enforced a racial hierarchy within the plants: Black workers were confined to the foundry, the hardest and most dangerous work, while white workers held the skilled and supervisory positions. When the Ford Motor Company promoted three Black workers to positions previously held only by whites in June 1943, 25,000 white workers walked off the job in a "hate strike."

On June 20, 1943, a fight broke out on Belle Isle, a recreational island in the Detroit River. Within hours, the violence had spread across the city. The riot lasted three days. 34 people were killed — 25 of them Black — and 433 were injured. Federal troops were deployed to end it. The causes: wartime competition for housing and jobs, racial restrictive covenants that made Black neighborhoods impossibly overcrowded, and a police department that arrested Black residents while allowing white mobs to operate freely. Of the 34 killed, 17 were killed by police — all 17 were Black.

The 1943 riot accelerated white flight from mixed neighborhoods, hardened residential segregation, and embedded a pattern of police impunity toward Black Detroiters that would continue for another three decades — until the 1967 uprising made it nationally impossible to ignore.

Motown · 1959 – 1972
3
1959 – 1972

Motown Records: The $800 Loan That Built the World's Biggest Black Music Empire

2648 West Grand Boulevard, Detroit ("Hitsville U.S.A.")

Berry Gordy Jr., a former assembly line worker at Ford's Lincoln-Mercury plant, founded Tamla Records in 1959 with an $800 family loan. He renamed it Motown Record Corporation — the Motor City label. His first office and recording studio was a house on West Grand Boulevard he called Hitsville U.S.A. His explicit strategy: create Black music that white radio stations would play. The Motown Sound — tight arrangements, call-and-response vocals, pop-accessible hooks layered over Detroit soul — was engineered for crossover. It worked.

Between 1961 and 1971, Motown had 110 Billboard top-ten hits. The roster: The Supremes (Diana Ross), The Temptations, The Four Tops, Marvin Gaye, Stevie Wonder, Smokey Robinson and the Miracles, Martha and the Vandellas, the Jackson 5. Gordy ran Motown like a Ford assembly line — songwriters, producers, and artists worked specific roles in a vertically integrated creative factory. The Funk Brothers, the studio band that played on virtually every Motown hit, were never credited on any album. They were salaried employees, paid session rates, while the records they played on sold tens of millions of copies.

Gordy moved Motown's headquarters to Los Angeles in 1972 — drawn by the film industry and the mainstream entertainment establishment. Detroit lost the label, and with it the infrastructure and professional ecosystem that had supported hundreds of musicians, producers, and studio workers. The Hitsville U.S.A. building is now a museum.

The Funk Brothers — the uncredited session musicians on virtually every Motown hit — were the subject of the 2002 documentary Standing in the Shadows of Motown, which finally gave them public recognition decades after their contributions. The pattern of Black creative labor generating wealth for Black-owned but hierarchically structured institutions — where the credit and the royalties flow upward — mirrors the broader extractive logic Black artists have always navigated.

The 1967 Uprising · America's Deadliest 20th-Century Urban Rebellion
4
July 23–28, 1967

The 1967 Detroit Uprising: Five Days That Broke the City Open

Detroit's Near West Side → citywide

At 3:35 a.m. on July 23, 1967, Detroit Police raided an unlicensed after-hours bar (blind pig) at 12th Street and Clairmount Avenue — a regular gathering spot for Black veterans celebrating the return of two servicemen from Vietnam. Police expected a small group; they found 82 people. The arrests took over an hour. A crowd gathered. Bottles were thrown. By dawn, the West Side was burning.

The 1967 Detroit uprising lasted five days and is the largest and deadliest urban rebellion in twentieth-century American history. 43 people were killed, 1,189 injured, 7,200 arrested, and 2,000 buildings were destroyed. Governor George Romney requested federal troops; President Johnson sent the 82nd and 101st Airborne Divisions — the same units that had been deployed in Vietnam. National Guardsmen fired indiscriminately into buildings. The Algiers Motel incident — in which Detroit police murdered three young Black men, Carl Cooper (17), Aubrey Pollard (19), and Fred Temple (18), in a motel annex during the uprising — became emblematic of the police's conduct. No officer was convicted.

The uprising's causes were not the blind pig raid. They were two decades of compressed grievance: police brutality, the destruction of Black Bottom, overcrowded segregated housing, unemployment (Black unemployment in Detroit was twice the white rate), and a police department — the Big Four tactical units — that operated as an occupation force in Black neighborhoods. The Kerner Commission, appointed by President Johnson to investigate the "riots," concluded in 1968 that "white racism" was the primary cause. The report was immediately shelved.

The 1967 uprising accelerated white flight that had already been underway. Detroit's white population, 1.5 million in 1950, fell to 400,000 by 2000. The tax base collapsed with it. The auto industry followed: GM, Ford, and Chrysler relocated plants to the suburbs, the South, and eventually overseas. The single largest driver of Detroit's fiscal collapse was not Black residents — it was the departure of white residents and their tax dollars, and the departure of the auto industry that had justified the city's existence.

Coleman Young & Political Power · 1973 – 1997
5
1973 – 1997

Coleman Young: Detroit's First Black Mayor and the War Against Him

Detroit City Hall

Coleman Young was elected Detroit's first Black mayor in November 1973, defeating the white police commissioner John Nichols in a campaign that split the city almost perfectly along racial lines. Young had been on the HUAC blacklist in 1952 for refusing to name names; he had organized Black workers in the UAW; he had served in the Tuskegee Airmen's ground crew. He took office knowing the city was structurally compromised: the tax base was eroding, the auto industry was consolidating, and the federal government was deliberately redirecting infrastructure investment to the suburbs.

Young's first major act was disbanding the STRESS unit (Stop the Robberies, Enjoy Safe Streets) — a plainclothes police decoy operation that had killed 22 people in its first two years, 20 of them Black. He diversified the police force, promoted Black officers, and redirected city investment toward Black neighborhoods that had been neglected for decades. The white political establishment — in suburban Detroit, in the state legislature, in Washington — treated every Young initiative as either incompetent or corrupt. State funding to Detroit was systematically reduced. The "Renaissance Center" corporate development on the riverfront, pushed by Ford's Henry Ford II, absorbed public money while the neighborhoods decayed.

Young served five terms — 20 years — and died in 1997. His tenure was marked by genuine achievements in Black political empowerment and genuine failures in economic policy, many of which were constrained by the structural conditions he inherited. He remains one of the most consequential and most unfairly maligned figures in American urban history.

"I issue an open warning now to all dope pushers, rip-off artists, and muggers: It's time to leave Detroit. Hit 8 Mile Road."

— Coleman Young, 1973 inaugural address
→ The Chain from Here

Young's successors inherited a city in accelerating structural decline. The 2013 Detroit municipal bankruptcy — the largest in American history — was the formal endpoint of a process that began with white flight and deindustrialization in the 1950s. The bankruptcy judge approved the sale of Detroit Institute of Arts assets before public pension cuts were negotiated — a decision that placed the city's cultural patrimony above the retirement security of its public workers, most of whom were Black.

The Water Crisis & Flint · 2014 – Present
6
2014 – Present

Flint: Environmental Racism as State Policy

Flint, Michigan (57% Black)

In 2013, the Michigan state government — acting under emergency manager legislation that stripped Flint's elected city government of authority — appointed an unelected emergency manager to run the city's finances. In April 2014, to save money, the emergency manager switched Flint's water supply from Detroit's treated Lake Huron water to the Flint River — without applying the federally required corrosion inhibitor treatment. The Flint River's chemistry corroded the city's aging lead pipes, leaching lead into the water supply. Flint's children drank lead-poisoned water for 18 months before state officials acknowledged the problem.

When a local pediatrician, Dr. Mona Hanna-Attisha, published data showing elevated blood-lead levels in Flint children in September 2015, state officials publicly disputed her findings. The EPA had known since June 2015. Governor Snyder's office had internal emails documenting the danger from July 2015. The cover-up continued until January 2016. By then, 6,000–12,000 children had been exposed to high lead levels. Lead poisoning in children causes irreversible neurological damage — lower IQ, behavioral problems, reduced impulse control. There is no cure.

Flint is a majority-Black city. The emergency manager system — which had also been imposed on Detroit, Pontiac, Benton Harbor, and other majority-Black Michigan cities — stripped elected local government from communities of color while leaving white-majority communities to self-govern. Environmental law scholars have described Flint as a textbook case of environmental racism: the systematic exposure of non-white communities to environmental hazards that white communities are protected from.

The city reached a $626 million settlement in 2021, most of which went to the children who were poisoned. Flint's water infrastructure has not been fully replaced as of 2025.

Aretha Franklin & the Spiritual Legacy
7
1942 – 2018

Aretha Franklin: The Queen from Detroit's New Bethel Baptist Church

New Bethel Baptist Church, Detroit

Aretha Franklin was born in Memphis but raised in Detroit, the daughter of the nationally prominent Baptist preacher C.L. Franklin, whose sermons were recorded and distributed on Chess Records. She learned to sing in the choir of New Bethel Baptist Church on Linwood Avenue — the church her father led for 35 years, the same church that became a major venue for civil rights organizing, hosting King and other movement leaders. Aretha's first recordings were Gospel, made at Columbia at 18. Her career did not break through until she signed with Atlantic Records in 1966 and producer Jerry Wexler recorded her at FAME Studios in Muscle Shoals, Alabama.

The result — I Never Loved a Man the Way I Love You (1967), followed by "Respect" (Otis Redding's song, rewritten and transformed by Aretha into something new) — made her the defining voice of soul music at the precise moment the civil rights movement was confronting its hardest questions. "Respect" became an anthem not just for Black America but for the women's movement. Franklin performed at the March on Washington and at King's funeral in 1968, where she sang "Precious Lord" — the Thomas Dorsey Gospel standard from Chicago — as his casket was carried out.

→ The Chain from Here

Franklin's voice ran through three generations of Detroit's Black sacred and secular tradition: from her father's church to Chess Records' Gospel distribution to Motown's commercial machine to Atlantic's soul moment. She is buried at Woodlawn Cemetery in Detroit, alongside her father. Her funeral in August 2018, at Greater Grace Temple, drew presidents, musicians, and dignitaries from across the world — back to the city that made her.

The City That Industry Built and Abandoned

Detroit didn't collapse because of its Black residents. It collapsed because the industry and the white tax base both left — and the state made sure the people left behind paid the price.

Paradise Valley was demolished for a freeway. The freeway is now being torn down. Flint's children were poisoned to save $15 a day in water treatment costs. The Motown Sound crossed over to every radio station in America while its creators worked for session wages. Detroit's story is the concentrated version of what industrial capitalism does to Black communities when the profits stop flowing.
